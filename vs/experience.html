Graph Methods for Particle Tracking

The High Luminosity - Large Hadron Collider is in need of efficient and fast particle track reconstruction as algorithms in use are unable to scale to current requirements. This project explores Graph Neural Networks as a solution to this problem.

We used the TrackML Codalab dataset with 9000 events and 100,000 hits from 10,000 particles. The TrackML pipeline comprises of multiple stages including processing, embedding, filtering, and GNN.

In the Processing stage, feature engineering was performed to select cluster features to construct events from TrackML dataset.

The Embedding pipeline involving graph construction was developed to find a good distance metric between pairs of 3D hit measurements wherein pairs belonging to same particles are nearby and further apart for different particles. A significant speedup was achieved in the training stage by adapting the Nearest Neighbors algorithm to make use of Fixed Radius Nearest Neighbors (FRNN) on CUDA. Benchmarking was performed to establish the superiority of the FRNN library over competing solutions such as Facebook's Faiss and PyTorch3D KNN. The optimal PyTorch based embedding model with 99% efficiency and 1% purity was generated through hyperparameter scanning.

NextGen Fellow, Citrine Informatics


Supervisor: Dane Morgan, Professor, Dept. Material Science and Engineering

Deep Learning for Automated Defect Recognition in Electron Microscopy Images

Classifying defects in metallic microstructures is done widely in the metallographic industry to understand and assess the  quality and performance of metal alloys. In nuclear applications this process is critical to understand the effects of radiation and design for safe operation of nuclear reactors.

Defects are frequently identified through electron microscopy, and the image analysis is done by hand, which is slow, error prone and inconsistent. Our project focused on automating the process using computer vision techniques based on convolution neural networks. The Univ. of WI team is exploring four implementations of networks, and this project is focused on one known as RetinaNet.

This project utlized the Keras implementation of RetinaNet. Hyperparameter optimization was performed to obtain the best model. An evaluation pipeline was developed to determine the recall and precision metrics. The detection model performed reasonably well with an average recall of 68% and average precision of 85% on the test set. Relatively low recall stems from inability to detect small defects.

I also co-authored a paper and presented the results at the NextGen Research Symposium in Golden, CO.
